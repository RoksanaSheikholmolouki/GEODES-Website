Current Team

Myriam Lemelin, Ph.D.

Associate professor
Departement of Applied Geomatics
University of Sherbrooke

Myriam is the Canada Research Chair in Northern and Planetary Geological Remote Sensing and professor in the Department of Applied Geomatics at the University of Sherbrooke. She obtained her Ph.D. from the University of Hawaii at Manoa, after completing a bachelor's degree in geomatics and a master's degree in geographic sciences - remote sensing at the University of Sherbrooke. She then completed a post-doctorate in spatial sciences at York University. Her research focuses on the remote sensing of the geology, ice, and soil properties of the Moon, asteroids, and Mars, supporting space exploration missions.

Guilhem Calas

Undergraduate student | Summer 2024 intern
Morphological study of lunar craters in the region of the Reiner Gamma magnetic anomaly.
Supervisor: Myriam Lemelin.

Born in France, Guilhem turned to geomatics after six years in computer science. He obtained a bachelor's degree in applied geomatics from the École Nationale de Sciences et Géographies in Paris, during which he completed an end-of-study internship with Myriam Lemelin's team. His internship project aims to study lunar regions with strong magnetic fields colocalized with albedo phenomena visible to the naked eye, as observed in the Reiner Gamma zone to be visited by the Vertex rover (NASA) in the coming years. He uses the ratio between crater depth and diameter to estimate crater age, then compares craters inside and outside the Reiner Gamma region. Guilhem will start a Master's degree in Myriam's team in January 2025.

Stéphanie Lachance

Master's student in applied geomatics and remote sensing (2020 - present)
Remote predictive mapping of gossans in volcanic terrain.
Research director: Myriam Lemelin.

Stéphanie does remote predictive mapping, a technique aimed at producing or improving geological maps using geospatial data. Geospatial data can be very varied: satellite images (Landsat, WorldView, Radarsat), geophysical surveys, digital elevation models, and so on. The aim of her master's project is to develop a remote predictive mapping method adapted to the geological formations and alterations present in the vicinity of the McGill Arctic Research Station (M.A.R.S.) on Axel Heiberg Island, Nunavut. The resulting geological map will delineate the gossans and paleo-hydrothermal alterations of the study site, all at a finer scale than the current geological map.
